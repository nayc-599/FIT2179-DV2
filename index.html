Understanding who arrives, who leaves, and what it means for the economy

Every year, hundreds of thousands of people move in and out of Australia. This page follows two decades of movement — from steady growth to the sudden stop of COVID-19 — and asks what happens to money when people cross borders.

3. Where in Australia Are They Leaving From?

Departures concentrate heavily in Australia's most populous, internationally connected states. New South Wales, Victoria and Queensland account for the vast majority of all departures, consistent with their populations and international connectivity. Every state shows the same sharp COVID collapse in 2021 — and a recovery that, years later, remains unfinished.

New South Wales accounts for the largest share of national departures — home to Sydney, Australia's most globally connected city and the first stop for many newly arrived migrants.

4. The Economic Angle — Remittances Over Time

Migration leaves an economic footprint long after the departure gate closes. The dual axis chart below places total overseas-born departures alongside Australia's personal remittances received across the same two decades. For two decades the lines tracked each other closely — until COVID-19 pulled them apart. Departures collapsed in 2021, yet remittances barely dipped before resuming their climb. The stacked line chart below then breaks this down by country, showing how IndiaIndia receives A$189B in remittances annually — a figure that grew sixfold since 2004, making it the world's largest remittance recipient by far.'s remittance income has grown sixfold since 2004 — dwarfing every other nation in this dataset.

Even as emigrant departures collapsed during COVID-19, remittances kept growing for most countries. The economic ties between Australia and its diaspora source nations proved far more resilient than the movement of people itself — money outlasts movement.
