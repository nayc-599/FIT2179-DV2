Who Comes, Who Goes: Australia's Migration Story

Understanding who arrives, who leaves, and what it means for the economy

Every year, hundreds of thousands of people move in and out of Australia. This page follows two decades of movement — from steady growth to the sudden stop of COVID-19 — and asks what happens to money when people cross borders.

1. Where Do Emigrants Come From?

Australia's emigrants span every continent, but the pattern is far from even. Asia accounts for roughly half of all overseas-born departures over the past two decades — a reflection of Australia's deep demographic and economic ties with the Asia-Pacific region.

Asia alone contributes more departures than Europe, Oceania, the Americas, Africa and the Middle East combined.

2. Who's Leaving the Most?

Zooming in from continents to countries, China, the United Kingdom and New Zealand top the list of emigrant sources over 21 years. Each country's story is different — some migrants retire abroad, others reunite with family, and many pursue opportunities in their country of birth.

China alone accounts for more than 590,000 departures — nearly double the next highest country, the United Kingdom.

3. How Have Rankings Changed Over Time?

These rankings were not always fixed. China's rise to the top was dramatic — from second place in 2014 to a dominant first by 2019. Then COVID-19 arrived and reshuffled everything. Press Play to watch the story unfold year by year.

Follow the coloured bands — when they cross, countries swap rank. The COVID year (2020–21) pulled every band toward the bottom at once.

4. Where in Australia Are They Leaving From?

Most emigrant departures happen from the states where most people live. New South Wales, Victoria and Queensland dominate the totals — but every state and territory contributes. The three largest states all show the same COVID dip, followed by a partial recovery.

New South Wales alone accounts for the largest share of national departures — consistent with it being Australia's most populous state.

5. The Economic Angle

Migration does not happen in isolation from money. The countries sending the most emigrants from Australia are also among the world's largest recipients of global remittances. The flow map shows where leavers were born — hover over a line to highlight that country's corridor.

High-departure countries in Asia cluster with the largest remittance flows — suggesting that migration and money move together, even when people are leaving rather than arriving.

6. Departures vs Remittances — A Continental View

Comparing departures and remittances side by side reveals a striking divergence. Asia dominates on both measures — but within Asia, India's remittance footprint far outweighs its share of Australian departures. The two metrics tell different stories about how countries connect to Australia.

India receives over $119 billion in remittances globally yet ranks only fourth in Australian departures — the largest gap between migration volume and economic footprint of any country in this dataset.
